1984 - Analysis

Plot Overview

George Orwell's novel 1984 presents a dystopian world in which the government, known as the Party, exercises total control over every aspect of life, including thought, language, and history. The story follows Winston Smith, a seemingly ordinary man who begins to question the reality constructed by the Party. As Winston becomes increasingly aware of the manipulation surrounding him, he attempts to rebel through private acts such as writing in a diary and forming a forbidden relationship. However, his resistance ultimately fails when he is captured and subjected to intense psychological conditioning. Through Winston's journey, Orwell explores the devastating effects of absolute power and warns readers about the dangers of unchecked authority.

Character List & Descriptions

Winston Smith: Winston Smith is the protagonist of the novel and serves as a representation of the struggle for individuality within a highly controlled society. He is thoughtful, observant, and deeply curious about the truth, particularly regarding the past and how it has been altered by the Party. His internal conflict revolves around his desire to resist the Party while also recognizing the danger of doing so. Throughout the novel, Winston attempts to preserve his sense of self through acts of rebellion, but his eventual downfall highlights the overwhelming power of the regime. His character ultimately demonstrates how even strong-willed individuals can be broken when faced with constant psychological pressure.

Julia: Julia is Winston's love interest and represents a different form of rebellion against the Party. Unlike Winston, she is not focused on intellectual or political resistance, but rather on reclaiming personal freedom through small acts of defiance. She values pleasure, emotion, and human connection, which the Party actively suppresses. Julia's practical approach to rebellion contrasts with Winston's idealism, showing that resistance can take multiple forms. However, her eventual betrayal under pressure reveals that even strong emotional bonds can be destroyed by fear and control.

O'Brien: O'Brien is one of the most complex characters in the novel, as he initially appears to be an ally to Winston but is later revealed to be a loyal member of the Party. He represents the intellectual and psychological power of the regime, using logic, manipulation, and torture to reshape Winston's understanding of reality. O'Brien's role highlights the Party's true objective: not just obedience, but complete control over thought and belief. His calm and calculated demeanor makes him particularly unsettling, as he genuinely believes in the Party's ideology.

Big Brother: Big Brother is the symbolic figurehead of the Party and represents constant surveillance, authority, and control. Although it is unclear whether Big Brother is a real person, his image is used to instill fear and loyalty among citizens. The phrase "Big Brother is watching you" reinforces the idea that individuals are always being monitored, which discourages independent thought and behavior. As a symbol, Big Brother demonstrates how power can be maintained through psychological influence rather than direct force.

Mr. Charrington: Mr. Charrington appears at first to be a harmless antique shop owner who offers Winston a glimpse into the past. However, his true identity as a member of the Thought Police reveals the extent of the Party's deception. His betrayal emphasizes the idea that the Party's control is inescapable and that even seemingly safe spaces are carefully monitored. Through his character, Orwell illustrates how trust can be manipulated in a society built on surveillance and control.

Chapter Summaries & Analysis

The novel begins with an introduction to Winston Smith, who lives in a totalitarian society where the Party, led by Big Brother, watches citizens' every move through the Thought Police and telescreens. He works at the Ministry of Truth, where he changes records in the past to match the Party's constantly changing version of the present and reality as a whole. Despite the constant surveillance and the risk of severe punishment, Winston begins secretly keeping a diary to express his thoughts and criticisms against the Party. Winston struggles to fully recall memories of his past as well as the general history of the world before the usurpation of control by the Party. Winston begins to analyze the people around him, such as the mindless Parsons family, Syme, his colleague, whom he deems too smart for his own good and soon to be "vaporized", and the dark-haired girl whom he simultaneously hates and lusts for, for he believes she is a part of the Thought Police. He is especially interested in his superior O'Brien, whom he believes might share his rebellious thoughts. Through his work and daily life, Winston becomes more aware of how deeply the Party controls truth, memory, and individual freedom.


Chapters 1-5 of Part One introduce the grasp of the Party, and how the Party's control impacts Winston by forcing him to hide his thoughts and live in constant fear of being caught for his minute acts of disobedience. Winston's overt rebellion officially begins with his first entry in his diary. Despite the insignificance of his silent rebellion, Winston acknowledges and accepts the fact that he will soon be apprehended and punished for his crimes, no matter the level of caution he practices. In contrast, characters like the Parsons and Syme show how deeply the Party has shaped people through blind, learned loyalty and manipulation. Winston's work rewriting history highlights how easily truth can be manipulated, revealing the Party's power over reality itself. Through this, we can learn that controlling language and information allows those in power to limit independent thinking. Orwell suggests that with a lack of personal thought and criticism, people lose their individuality and ability to challenge authority, allowing those in charge to shape reality and lead with total control.

Chapter 6-8
In Chapters 6-8 of Part One, Winston reflects on a past relationship with his ex-wife, Katherine, and begins to reflect on how the Party has removed intimacy and genuine emotion from relationships. He remembers that their marriage was purely for duty to the Party, not love. Winston becomes increasingly frustrated with the lack of truth in society and wishes he could connect with the proletarians (commonly referred to as proles), whom he believes may be the only hope for rebellion. He visits a prole neighbourhood and has a brief conversation with an old man, hoping to learn about the past, but finds that the man's memories are unclear and unreliable. Winston then enters a small antique shop owned by Mr. Charrington, where he buys a glass paperweight and feels a sense of connection to the past. While leaving, he sees the dark-haired girl again and becomes paranoid that she may be spying on him, leading to his belief that he has already been compromised and that suicide is the best option for him.

Chapters 6-8 of Part One show how the Party's control affects Winston by damaging his ability to form meaningful relationships, leaving him isolated and frustrated. His memories of Katherine highlight how even personal connections are controlled, reducing marriage to a service or duty to the Party rather than a source of love or comfort. This reveals that Winston values real human connection and truth, making him different from most citizens who accept the Party's rules and have no drive for natural human instinct. His attempt to learn from the old man shows his desire to understand the past, but also reveals how deeply the Party has erased reliable history not only from the books, but from the minds of the common people. The proles appear as a possible source of hope, yet their lack of awareness suggests they are unlikely to rebel. Through Winston's experiences, we can learn that without access to truth or veritable memory, people cannot effectively challenge those in power. Orwell suggests that controlling both personal relationships and historical knowledge allows a power to maintain total control over its subjects.
Chapterrs 1-5 p2
In Chapters 1-5 of Part Two, Winston's life greatly changes when the dark-haired girl, whom he first believed to be a spy, covertly gives him a note that says "I love you". This shocks him and reignites his will to live, and they eventually manage to have a brief discussion. They arrange to meet in the countryside, where they begin a presumably hidden relationship away from the Party's surveillance. Julia reveals that she has secretly rebelled against the Party for years, though she is less concerned with the downfall of the Party and more so on her own small acts of disobedience. Their relationship gives Winston a sense of freedom and happiness he has never experienced before. They later begin meeting in a room above Mr. Charrington's shop, rented by Winston, believing it to be a safe private space. The two grow closer, sharing thoughts and dreams of rebellion against the Party in their own, differing ways.
Chapters 1-5 of Part Two show how Winston is impacted by his long-awaited experience of genuine love and rebellion, which temporarily frees him from the Party's psychological control. His relationship with Julia allows him to feel human again, giving him hope and a sense of individuality that was previously neglected. This reveals that Winston deeply values truth, emotion, and connection, while Julia is more practical and focused on small and personal acts of rebellion rather than larger political change. Their different perspectives highlight contrasting ways people resist oppression and express their true selves. Their rented room symbolizes a temporary escape from the Party and their structured lives, but also shows how desperate they are for privacy in a heavily surveilled society. Through their relationship, we can learn that human connection can act as a form of resistance and comfort when dealing with an inhuman power. Orwell suggests, however, that even these moments of freedom are delicate in a society where surveillance and control are constant.
Chapters 6-8 p2
In Chapters 6-8 of Part Two, Winston encounters O'Brien, who invites him to his apartment under the pretense of discussing a dictionary worked on by the now late Syme, indicating his ideological alignment with Winston. Winston and Julia visit O'Brien together and declare their willingness to rebel against the Party, even if it means committing extreme acts. After this, O'Brien reveals that he is part of the Brotherhood, an underground resistance group against the party, and Winston feels his suspicions confirmed. O'Brien gives Winston instructions to receive a copy of a forbidden book written by Emmanuel Goldstein, the face of the Brotherhood, which explains the Party's structure and ideology. Winston also reflects on the freedom of the proles relative to the people of the Party, stating that the proles are more human than he and Julia are. This leads to their shared thought that they should probably abandon the room, but both of them, after having experienced the bliss of privacy and intimacy, cannot bring themselves to do so.


Chapters 6-8 of Part Two show how Winston is affected by the belief that he has finally found others who oppose the Party, giving him a sense of hope and purpose that makes him more willing to take risks. His meeting with O'Brien and his decision to join the Brotherhood demonstrate how far he is willing to go, even agreeing to extreme actions, revealing his determination to fight for change. This suggests that Winston is idealistic and eager to believe in rebellion, even if he may not see the result of it, as he places trust in O'Brien despite the danger and lack of certainty. Julia's participation highlights her loyalty to Winston, but also shows that her rebellion is more inclined to personal desire than to political ideology. Winston's reflection on the proles reveals his growing belief that true freedom may exist outside the Party, yet it also emphasizes his frustration that they are not actively resisting. Through this, we can learn that oppression can persist not only through control but also through a lack of unity and awareness among those who could challenge it. Orwell suggests that even when individuals recognize the truth, their desire for comfort or connection can prevent them from making the choices necessary for real change.
Chapters 9-10 p2
In Chapters 9-10 of Part Two, Winston finally receives and reads Emmanuel Goldstein's book, which explains how the Party maintains power through constant war, control of information, and the manipulation of truth. The book describes the structure of society and how the Party keeps citizens oppressed by preventing independent thought. As Winston reads, he feels validated, realizing that his suspicions about the Party's operations are correct. He reads a bit of the book to Julia, though she is, expectedly, less interested in the workings of the party. As they both lie in bed, they feel secure and comfortable together. However, this sense of safety is quickly broken when a hidden telescreen behind a painting is revealed in the room. Mr. Charrington is exposed as a member of the Thought Police, and Winston and Julia are arrested.

Chapters 9-10 of Part Two show how Winston is affected by the knowledge of the Party's true methods, which strengthens his belief that rebellion is justified. Reading Goldstein's book gives him confidence that he understands the system, but also frustrates him with the lack of understanding of the motives behind the system and the Party as a whole. This reveals that Winston is intellectually driven and seeks truth, yet he underestimates the extent of the Party's control. Julia's lack of interest in the book highlights her focus on personal rebellion rather than understanding the larger system. The sudden betrayal by Mr. Charrington shows how deeply the Party infiltrates every aspect of life, destroying any illusion of safety. Through this, we can learn that knowledge alone is not enough to defeat oppression, especially when those in power control every aspect of life. Orwell suggests that in a totalitarian society, even moments of hope and understanding can be manipulated and used against individuals.


Chapters 1-3 part 3
In Chapters 1-3 of Part Three, Winston is taken to the Ministry of Love, where he is locked up and subjected to harsh physical and psychological torture. He is repeatedly beaten and questioned by guards and researchers until he is completely exhausted and weakened. Eventually, O'Brien appears, revealing that he has been loyal to the Party all along and is now in charge of Winston's interrogation. O'Brien begins "re-educating" Winston, forcing him to accept the Party's version of reality, even when it contradicts logic or evidence, pushing the idea that it is Winston who is the insane one for his beliefs and disbeliefs. Winston struggles to resist but is gradually broken down through pain and manipulation, acknowledging that human reasoning falls before physical pain. He is forced to accept ideas like 2 + 2 = 5, demonstrating the Party's control over truth and perception. Throughout this process, Winston begins to lose his sense of self and his ability to distinguish reality from the Party's imposed beliefs.
Chapters 1-3 of Part Three show how Winston is affected by the Party's torture, which breaks him down through both psychological and physical means until he can no longer trust his own thoughts. The constant and endless pain and manipulation force him to abandon his own logic and accept whatever the Party tells him, demonstrating the extent of the control the Party has over him. This reveals that Winston, although once rebellious and independent, is ultimately vulnerable when faced with overwhelming force such as the Party. O'Brien's role highlights the Party's true goal, which is not just obedience, but total control over a person's mind and their idea of reality. The idea that Winston must believe statements like 2 + 2 = 5, and later 2 + 2 equals whatever the Party says, shows how the Party rewrites truth itself, making reality, even if objective, meaningless. Through this, we can learn that a force with absolute power can reshape not only actions but also beliefs, leaving individuals with no sense of identity. Orwell suggests that when authority controls both pain and truth, resistance becomes nearly impossible, as even human reasoning can be overridden.

Chapters 3-6 p3
In Chapters 3-6 of Part Three, Winston begins to physically recover from the abuse while continuing his "re-education," and O'Brien shifts from physical torture and pain to a more psychologically manipulative approach. O'Brien explains the Party's true goal and motive, which is not just material power, but complete power over reality and the human mind. He tells Winston that the Party seeks power for its own sake, rejecting Winston's flattering ideas of justice or equality. Winston still clings to his love for Julia as a final act of resistance, believing that the Party cannot control his feelings, and that true freedom was to die rejecting the sole purpose of his torture, to despise the Party and Big Brother. However, in Room 101, Winston is confronted with his greatest fear, rats, and is pushed to the breaking point. In his terror, he betrays Julia, begging for the pain to be inflicted on her instead of himself. By the end, Winston is released back into society, completely broken, and eventually comes to love Big Brother.

Chapters 3-6 of Part Three show how Winston is impacted by the Party's final stage of control, as he is not only physically abused, but has his mind reformed until he loses all resistance. O'Brien's shift to psychological manipulation demonstrates that true power lies in controlling how individuals think and perceive reality, not just how they act. This reveals that Winston's earlier confidence in his ability to resist was ultimately doomed, as even his strongest belief, his love for Julia, was destroyed under extreme fear. O'Brien also reveals the Party's true nature, showing that it seeks power purely for its own sake at the highest ranks, which highlights the emptiness and cruelty of its ideology. Winston's betrayal of Julia in Room 101 exposes how fear can override loyalty, morality, and identity, breaking even the deepest human bonds. Through this, we can learn that when facing a power with absolute control, even personal emotions and relationships can be manipulated or erased. Orwell suggests that when authority gains complete power over both the mind and the body, it can force individuals to accept and even love their own oppression, living in blissful ignorance.

Theme & Universal Idea

The central theme of 1984 is the manipulation and control of truth. Orwell demonstrates how those in power can alter reality by controlling information, language, and historical records. This control prevents individuals from thinking critically or questioning authority, ultimately stripping them of their independence. The theme extends beyond the novel, as it reflects real-world concerns about propaganda, misinformation, and the influence of authority on public perception. The ending of the novel reinforces the idea that without awareness and resistance, individuals can lose not only their freedom but also their ability to distinguish truth from falsehood.

Figurative Devices & Importance

Motif (Surveillance): Surveillance is a recurring motif throughout the novel, appearing in the form of telescreens, hidden microphones, and the constant presence of the Thought Police. This motif emphasizes the lack of privacy experienced by citizens and reinforces the idea that individuals are always being watched. As a result, people begin to monitor their own behavior, demonstrating how control can be internalized. This contributes to Winston's paranoia and isolation, highlighting the psychological effects of living under constant observation.

Symbolism (Big Brother): Big Brother functions as a powerful symbol of authority and control within the novel. His image is used to represent the Party's dominance and to remind citizens of their lack of freedom. Even though his existence is uncertain, the impact of his presence is very real. This symbolism shows how power can be maintained through fear and belief rather than physical enforcement, making it even more effective.

Diction and Tone: Orwell's use of harsh, bleak, and oppressive language creates a tone of hopelessness throughout the novel. Words associated with control, fear, and restriction dominate the narrative, reinforcing the atmosphere of the society Winston lives in. This tone helps readers understand the emotional weight of the characters' experiences and emphasizes the severity of the Party's influence on everyday life.

Important Quote

"Big Brother is watching you."

This quote is one of the most recognizable lines from the novel and appears repeatedly as a slogan used by the Party. It serves as a constant reminder to citizens that they are under surveillance at all times, influencing their behavior and thoughts. The quote is significant not only because of its literal meaning, but also because of its psychological impact. It creates a sense of fear and self-awareness, forcing individuals to conform even when they are alone. Additionally, the repetition of this phrase throughout the novel reinforces the theme of control and highlights how language can be used as a tool to maintain power. Through this quote, Orwell demonstrates that authority can extend beyond physical enforcement and into the minds of individuals.

Key Questions & Answers

1. How does the Party maintain control over individuals?
The Party maintains control by manipulating truth, controlling information, and using fear to suppress independent thought. By rewriting history and limiting knowledge, it prevents people from questioning authority and ensures obedience.

2. What does Winston's downfall reveal about power?
Winston's downfall demonstrates that absolute power can break even the strongest individuals. It reveals that when authority controls both physical actions and mental processes, resistance becomes nearly impossible.
